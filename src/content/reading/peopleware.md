---
title: "Peopleware: Productive Projects and Teams (3rd Edition)"
author: "Tom DeMarco & Timothy Lister"
rating: 3
order: 2
review: |
  Peopleware is considered a classic in software development and management literature, and it’s easy to see why. The central message—that people, culture, and the working environment have a greater impact on productivity than processes or tools—remains relevant today.

  That said, I found parts of the book quite dated. A significant portion focuses on office layouts, workplace interruptions, and creating productive physical environments. While these topics were undoubtedly important when the book was written, they felt less applicable to me, having spent most of my career working in remote and hybrid environments.

  Despite that, the book offers an interesting perspective on how teams were managed in the past and why certain management practices emerged. Many of the underlying principles still hold true: trust matters, teams need space to think, and productivity is rarely improved through tighter control or more meetings.

  What I found most valuable wasn’t the specific recommendations, but the broader reminder that software development is ultimately a human activity. Successful products are built by motivated people working in healthy environments, not by processes alone.

  While I wouldn’t consider this a must-read for every modern Product Manager, I can see why it has influenced generations of leaders and practitioners. It’s a useful book for understanding the history of software management and many of the ideas that continue to shape team culture today.

  Would I recommend it? Yes, but with context. Read it as a classic that provides historical perspective and timeless lessons about people and teams, rather than as a practical guide for today’s workplace.
