In practice, organisations rarely run Lean and Six Sigma separately. They combine them as **Lean Six Sigma**: use Lean to strip out waste and make work flow, and Six Sigma to make what's left consistent. It's the single most common "process excellence" methodology you'll meet — and it comes with a ladder of certifications.

## The belts

Skill levels borrow martial-arts belt colours. Higher belt = deeper training and bigger projects:

<BeltLadder caption="The belt ladder. Green is the level that carries genuine weight for a PM — and it's well backed by QA/process experience." />

- **White** — basic awareness of the concepts. A weak signal on its own.
- **Yellow** — foundational understanding; fast and cheap. Enough to clear a "Lean" keyword filter on a job application.
- **Green** — can *lead* a DMAIC improvement project. This is the level that demonstrates real, applied capability.
- **Black** (and **Master Black**) — full-time improvement expert who coaches others. Usually overkill for a product manager.

## Who certifies it

Because Lean Six Sigma is open (no single owner), the *body* behind a certificate is what signals credibility. Recognised ones include **ASQ** (the gold standard — requires a verified real project for Green/Black), **IASSC** (exam-based, no project), and **CSSC** (accessible, free self-study, paid exam). For the EU/Ukraine market, prefer an internationally recognised body over an obscure provider.

## What's worth certifying — for a PM

- **Green Belt** if you'll back it with real process work (your QA Lead experience legitimately does). It's the level that carries weight.
- **Yellow Belt** if you just need to tick the keyword box quickly and cheaply.
- **Black Belt** generally isn't worth it for a PM — it's aimed at full-time improvement specialists.

## The PM takeaway

A Green Belt says "I can run a structured, data-driven improvement and make it stick" — which, paired with your quality background, is a credible, low-cost signal. Just don't over-invest: a belt is a small CV line, not the thing that gets you hired.
