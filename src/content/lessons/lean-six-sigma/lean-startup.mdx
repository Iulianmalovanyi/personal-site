The operations side (the last three lessons) makes a *known* process better. **Lean Startup** tackles a different waste entirely: the waste of carefully building a product when you don't yet know if anyone wants it. This is the "Lean" that's really your home turf as a PM.

## The core idea: don't build the wrong thing

In a startup or a new product, the biggest risk usually isn't *can we build it?* — it's *should we?* Lean Startup answers that by treating every plan as a set of **assumptions to test**, as cheaply and quickly as possible, before committing to a full build.

## The engine: build-measure-learn

<BuildMeasureLearn caption="The loop that drives Lean Startup. The goal isn't to go around it once — it's to go around it fast, and as many times as it takes to learn." />

- **Build** the smallest thing that tests your riskiest assumption — an **MVP**.
- **Measure** how real users actually behave with it (not what they say in a survey).
- **Learn** from the data, then decide: keep going, or change course.

The metric of progress here is **validated learning** — what you've *proven* about customers — not the number of features shipped. A quarter where you built less but learned the thing that saves a year of wasted work is a good quarter.

## MVP — the most misunderstood term

An **MVP** is *not* "version 1 with the nice bits removed." It's the **smallest experiment that answers your scariest question.** Sometimes that's a rough product; sometimes it's a landing page, a concierge mock-up, or a clickable prototype. The test is: *will this teach me what I need to know with the least effort?*

## Pivot or persevere

Each loop ends in a decision: **persevere** (the evidence supports the direction — push on) or **pivot** (the evidence says change a core assumption — product, audience, or model). A pivot isn't failure; it's the system working.

## Why you don't certify this

Unlike the operations side, Lean Startup has **no respected certificate** — paid "Lean Startup" badges read as box-buying. Employers want to see it *practised.* The good news: you already do it. Describe it in your own words on your CV — for example:

- "Ran hypothesis-driven discovery and build-measure-learn cycles; validated capabilities via MVPs before committing to full build."
- "Made pivot-or-persevere roadmap calls based on data, clinical feedback and real-world usage."

## The net for a PM

One paid certificate — a Six Sigma **belt** — credibly covers the operations side. The product side is covered by your **experience and how you describe it**, not a badge. Know which "Lean" a conversation is about, and you'll always reach for the right one.
