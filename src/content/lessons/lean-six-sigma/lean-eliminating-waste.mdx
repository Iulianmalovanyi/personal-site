Lean is, at heart, one stubborn question asked over and over: *does this step add value the customer would pay for — and if not, why are we doing it?* Everything else is technique.

## Where it came from

Lean grew out of the **Toyota Production System** in post-war Japan, where capital and materials were scarce and waste was unaffordable. Western writers later named the approach "Lean" and distilled it into five principles. The core promise: **deliver more value to customers using less of everything** — less time, less inventory, less effort.

## The five principles

1. **Define value** — precisely, from the customer's point of view.
2. **Map the value stream** — lay out every step from raw input to delivered value, and mark which steps actually add value.
3. **Make it flow** — remove the stops, queues and handoffs so work moves smoothly.
4. **Let the customer pull** — produce in response to real demand, not forecasts.
5. **Pursue perfection** — repeat, improving continuously (**kaizen**).

## The eight wastes

Lean gives waste concrete categories so teams can spot it. A common mnemonic is **DOWNTIME**:

The point isn't to memorise the list — it's the habit: look at any workflow and ask *where is the waiting, the rework, the moving things around, the half-used skills?*

## Tools you'll hear named

- **Value-stream mapping** — drawing the end-to-end process to expose delay and waste.
- **5S** — a workplace-organisation method (sort, set in order, shine, standardise, sustain) for tidy, efficient, repeatable work.
- **Kanban** — a visual board that signals what to pull next and limits work-in-progress.
- **Just-in-time (JIT)** — make/order only what's needed, when it's needed.

## Why it matters for a PM

You won't run a value-stream-mapping workshop, but you'll be *in the conversation*. When an operations lead says "there's too much WIP and we're overproducing," you'll know they mean work is being pushed in batches and piling up — and you can help re-sequence the roadmap so the team builds to real demand. Lean is also the vocabulary behind a lot of "ways of working" discussions well beyond the factory floor.
