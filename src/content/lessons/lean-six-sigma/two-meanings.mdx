If you only remember one thing from this topic, make it this: **"Lean" is used for two different things.** They share a name and a mindset, but they solve different problems, suit different teams, and certify completely differently. Job descriptions cheerfully list both in the same sentence, which is exactly why people get them muddled.

## The two meanings

<LeanSplit caption="Same word, two worlds. One improves how you make things; the other helps you decide what to make." />

- **Lean & Six Sigma — the operations side.** This is about taking an *existing* process — a production line, a support workflow, a release pipeline — and making it **faster (Lean: less waste)** and **more consistent (Six Sigma: less variation)**. It comes from manufacturing, it's data-driven, and it has a formal skill ladder you can certify with a "belt."
- **Lean Startup — the product side.** This is about *not wasting effort building the wrong product*. You figure out what to build through small experiments — **build, measure, learn** — instead of betting everything on a big upfront plan. It comes from the startup world, and there's no respected certificate for it.

## What they share

Both grew from the same instinct, which is why they share the word:

- **Cut waste.** Lean cuts waste from a process; Lean Startup cuts the waste of building features nobody wants.
- **Chase value.** Both define everything around what the customer actually values, and treat the rest as overhead.

That shared philosophy is real — but it's where the similarity ends.

## Why the distinction matters to a PM

- **Clarity in the room.** When someone says "let's be lean about this," knowing which they mean — tighten the process, or test the idea cheaply — keeps you from talking past each other.
- **It decides what to certify.** The operations side is worth a certificate (next lessons explain the belts). The product side is not — you show it through experience. Getting that backwards wastes money on a low-value badge.
- **You already do one of them.** As a PM running discovery, MVPs and roadmap pivots, you *practise* Lean Startup daily. Naming it precisely lets you claim it credibly.

The next three lessons take the operations side in turn — Lean, then Six Sigma, then the belts — and the final lesson returns to Lean Startup.
