Launch isn't the finish line; it's roughly the *start* of the product's real life. Three stages follow, and the last instinct to unlearn from software lives here.

## Ramp — scaling up volume

Once PVT proves the line, you **ramp** to full **mass production**. The product already works and can be built; the new problems are *business* problems at volume: forecasting how many to make, securing the supply chain, and holding quality steady as output climbs. A component shortage or a creeping defect rate now hits every unit.

## Sustaining — life in the field

This is usually the longest stage, and where a connected product earns its keep. The work splits in two:

- **Software/firmware:** ship improvements and fixes via **OTA updates**. Because a bad update can **brick** (disable) thousands of devices at once, *staged rollout* and *rollback* matter even more than in app-land.
- **Hardware:** you can't update it remotely. A change to the electronics or enclosure becomes a new **rev** (revision) — rev A, B, C — and every unit already in customers' hands stays exactly as it was built.

Alongside that, you watch **field quality** (a spike in **RMAs** — returns — is an early warning), and you run **cost-down**: trimming a few cents of unit cost is an ongoing product activity, because every cent is multiplied by every unit, forever.

## The two clocks

That OTA-versus-rev split creates the defining rhythm of connected-product PM: **two clocks running at once.**

<TwoClocks caption="Hardware refreshes slowly, in revisions years apart; the software and cloud layer iterates fast on top. You manage both at once, across a fleet of mixed hardware revisions." />

Your software roadmap moves weekly; your hardware roadmap moves every year or few. And because old revs stay in the field, your software often has to keep working across *several* hardware versions at the same time. Planning a feature means asking not just "can we build it?" but "will it run on rev A units too?"

## End of life

Eventually a product is retired. **EOL** isn't just switching off a page — it's a **last-time-buy** of components before suppliers stop making them, planning spares and support for units still in the field, and migrating customers onto whatever's next. Done badly, you strand paying customers; done well, it's an orderly hand-off.

## The PM takeaway

After launch, your job shifts from *getting it built* to *keeping a fleet healthy*: rolling out software safely, watching field quality, squeezing cost, and planning hardware revisions and retirement — all while remembering that some of your decisions (the software ones) are reversible and some (the hardware ones) are not.
