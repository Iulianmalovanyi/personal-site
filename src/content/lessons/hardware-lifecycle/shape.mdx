A software product can feel like it has no fixed shape — you ship, learn, and ship again, often the same day. A physical product is different. It moves through a **fixed sequence of stages**, mostly in order, and each one is harder and more expensive to undo than the last. Learning that sequence is the single most useful map you can carry into a hardware conversation.

## From idea to retirement

Almost every connected product walks the same path. It helps to group it into three acts:

- **Define & design** — figure out what to build, what it should cost, how it looks, and how all the parts (electronics, software, mechanics) fit together.
- **Validate** — build real prototypes and prove, in stages, that the design *works*, *meets its spec and passes certification*, and *can actually be manufactured*.
- **Make & support** — ramp up volume production, keep the product healthy in the field, and eventually retire it.

You'll meet the individual stage names — and the famous acronyms **EVT, DVT, PVT** — in the next two lessons. For now, the shape is the point.

## A gate between every stage

Between stages sits a **stage gate**: a checkpoint where the team asks "is this good enough to move on?" A gate isn't a calendar date — it's a *go/no-go decision*. Passing it usually unlocks the next, more expensive commitment: ordering long-lead components, cutting tooling, booking a certification lab.

That makes gates the natural place for a PM to do their highest-value work: holding the line on requirements, deciding what's good enough, and choosing — deliberately — when to spend the next chunk of money and time.

## Why hardware front-loads its hardest decisions

In software, you can defer decisions cheaply: build a bit, learn, change it. Hardware punishes that instinct for three reasons:

- **Change is expensive and often irreversible.** There's no hotfix for a fabricated circuit board or a cut steel mould. Decisions lock in early and hard.
- **Lead times dominate the schedule.** Components, board fabrication, tooling and certification each take weeks to months, and they run in series — one waits for the next. That chain is your **critical path**, and you can't compress it by working harder.
- **Money goes out before any product comes in.** Much of the cost is spent up front, long before the first unit ships.

Put together, this flips the software habit on its head: instead of deferring decisions until you have more information, you **front-load the de-risking** — you spend effort early to retire the scariest unknowns *before* they get expensive to fix.

## What this means for you as a PM

You don't need to design the circuit or run the factory. You need to know *where you are on this timeline*, *what the next gate commits you to*, and *which risks must be killed before that gate* — so the team spends its money and its precious lead time on the right things, in the right order.
