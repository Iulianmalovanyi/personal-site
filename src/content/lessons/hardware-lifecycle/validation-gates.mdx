Once a design exists, you don't just "go to production." You prove it in three escalating stages, each building real prototypes and asking a sharper question. They're known everywhere by their initials — **EVT, DVT, PVT** — and they always run in this order, because each one assumes the previous question is already answered.

## EVT — does it work?

**Engineering Validation Test** is the first batch of genuinely working prototypes. The question is the most basic one: *does the thing function at all?* Expect the fundamental design flaws to surface here — a power rail that sags, a radio that won't reach, parts that don't physically fit.

**The PM's job:** triage what's broken, decide what must be fixed versus tolerated, and *hold the spec* against the temptation to quietly drop a requirement that turned out to be hard.

## DVT — does it meet spec and pass certs?

**Design Validation Test** uses a near-final design and asks a tougher question: *does it meet the full specification, and does it pass the tests required to sell it?* This is where formal **V&V** and reliability testing happen, and crucially where **certification** is driven.

<Callout type="warning" title="Regulatory detail — verify with a specialist before relying on it">
<p>To sell a connected device in the EU you generally need <strong>CE marking</strong>, and anything with a radio also falls under the <strong>RED</strong> (Radio Equipment Directive), alongside electromagnetic-compatibility (<strong>EMC</strong>) and materials rules (<strong>RoHS/REACH</strong>). The point for a PM: these are non-negotiable gates, the testing takes weeks, and it can fail — so it sits squarely on the critical path. The exact requirements change and are consequential; confirm specifics with a regulatory/QA specialist.</p>
</Callout>

**The PM's job:** drive certification early enough that a failure doesn't blow the launch date, and protect the reliability bar.

## PVT — can the line build it?

**Production Validation Test** is a small run built on the *real* manufacturing line, with real tooling and process. The product already works (EVT) and meets spec (DVT); now the question is about the *factory*: *can it build this consistently, at quality, at an acceptable **yield**?* (Yield = the share of units that come off the line good.)

**The PM's job:** validate yield and assembly quality before committing to volume. Low yield is "margin death" — if one unit in five is scrap, the four good ones have to pay for it.

## Why the order is fixed

You can't validate manufacturing (PVT) of a design that doesn't yet meet spec (DVT), and you can't certify a design that doesn't yet work (EVT). Each gate stands on the one before. When you hear a hardware team say "we slipped DVT," they're telling you the *meets-spec-and-passes-certs* question isn't answered yet — and that everything downstream just moved.
