Every lifecycle gate you've met runs on a handful of money-and-manufacturing words. You don't need to calculate them — you need to *use them correctly* and know which lever each one pulls. Here they are, grouped by what they actually do to your product.

## Two kinds of cost

The first thing to get straight: hardware has **one-time costs** and **per-unit costs**, and they behave completely differently.

<CostBreakdown caption="One-time costs (NRE) are paid once, before units ship. Per-unit cost (BOM) repeats for every unit. Together they shape COGS — and the price." />

- **NRE (non-recurring engineering)** is paid *once*, before a single unit ships: the engineering effort, the **tooling** (the moulds and fixtures a factory needs — often expensive to cut), and certification. Make 100 units or 100,000 — the NRE is the same.
- **BOM (bill of materials)** is the parts list for *one* unit, with each part's price. Add it up, plus assembly, test and packaging, and you get **COGS (cost of goods sold)** — the cost of one finished unit. This repeats for every unit, forever.

That split explains a lot of hardware behaviour. High NRE wants high volume to spread it thin. High BOM cost can't be volume'd away — it's baked into every unit, which is why **cost-down** (shaving cents off the BOM) is a permanent activity, and why the **COGS target** is set so early in design.

### How volume changes the maths

A quick, hypothetical illustration (numbers invented to show the *shape*, not real figures):

- Say one-time **NRE** is **$100,000**, and each unit's **BOM** is **$20**.
- Build **1,000** units → the NRE adds **$100** per unit → effective cost ≈ **$120**.
- Build **100,000** units → the NRE adds just **$1** per unit → effective cost ≈ **$21**.

Same product, wildly different unit economics — purely from volume spreading the fixed NRE. It's why hardware businesses obsess over volume, and why a high-NRE design can be smart at scale and ruinous for a niche product.

## Words that gate your timing and volume

- **Lead time** — the wait to receive something you ordered. Components, board fabrication, tooling and certification each carry weeks-to-months of lead time, and they chain together into the critical path. This is why hardware can't "sprint."
- **MOQ (minimum order quantity)** — the smallest batch a supplier will produce. MOQs force you to commit to volume and cash *before* you know real demand, turning forecasting into a financial bet.
- **NPI (new product introduction)** — the structured hand-off from a finished design into volume manufacturing. It's the umbrella name for getting cleanly through the EVT→DVT→PVT→ramp sequence with a contract manufacturer.

## Words that quietly eat margin

- **Yield** — the fraction of units that come off the line good. Low yield means you're paying to build scrap; the good units carry that cost.
- **RMA (return merchandise authorization)** — the process for customers returning faulty units. A rising RMA rate is both a direct cost and an early warning of a field-quality problem.
- **DFM (design for manufacturing)** — designing so the product can be built reliably and cheaply at volume (met in lesson 2). Good DFM is what *protects* yield and BOM cost before they become problems.

## The PM takeaway

When you can say "our **NRE** is heavy because of **tooling**, so we need volume to hit our **COGS target**, but our **MOQ** and component **lead times** mean we commit cash before demand is proven, and if **yield** at **PVT** is low it'll eat the margin" — and mean it — you're speaking hardware. That sentence touches design, validation, manufacturing and unit economics at once, which is exactly the cross-disciplinary view a hardware PM is hired for.
