A connected product isn't one thing — it's a **stack of layers**, each built by a different team, each changing at a different speed. Internalise this picture and almost everything else about hardware falls into place.

## The five layers

<LayerStack caption="Five layers, top to bottom. Each is owned by a different team — and the deeper you go, the more a change costs and the slower it moves." />

From the user inward:

- **App (mobile / web)** — what the user taps. Pure software; ships in days.
- **Cloud / backend** — stores state, manages the fleet of devices, runs analytics, pushes updates.
- **Firmware / embedded** — the software that runs *on the device*. Updatable over the air, but carefully.
- **Electronics** — the physical brains: the **MCU** (microcontroller), sensors, the circuit board, power.
- **Mechanical / enclosure** — the case and physical form. Locked in once the factory tooling is cut.

## The one rule worth memorising

> **Change cost rises, and cadence slows, as you go down the stack.**

- The **app** team can ship on a Friday afternoon.
- The **firmware** team ships cautiously — a bad over-the-air update can disable thousands of devices at once.
- The **electronics** can't change at all after the board is made without a **respin** — weeks and money.
- The **mechanical** layer is frozen once tooling is cut — months and serious money.

## What it changes for a PM

This single rule does a lot of work:

- **It tells you who owns a problem.** "The reading is wrong" could live in any layer — knowing the stack tells you where to look.
- **It tells you how reversible a decision is.** A software choice is cheap to revisit; a mechanical one is essentially permanent.
- **It tells you the order to work in.** You de-risk the slow, expensive bottom layers *first* — the opposite of the software reflex to defer and iterate.

Keep this stack in your head and the next lesson — how data actually moves through it — will feel obvious.
